I am Sitting in a Room (in Indiranagar)
Alvin Lucier’s famous 1969 work, I Am Sitting in a Room, sets up an elegant system of resonance and decay: Lucier records himself reading the titular text, which is then played back into the room and re-recorded. With each successive pass, his voice dissolves further into a drone of the room’s resonant frequencies. The piece can be seen to efface subjectivity or convey its situated, relational nature. The work can be considered as an aural palimpsest in which an audio effect produced by a physical space is gradually compounded until the original recorded material is rendered unrecognisable. Despite the gradual foregrounding of the effect of the artist’s environment, the text of the work does not acknowledge its wider context beyond merely “a room”. In attempting to perform and record the work in a wholly different context, such as a room in Indiranagar, the differences between Lucier’s environment and mine become discernible. In I am Sitting in a Room, the only two actors are Lucier’s voice and the room he is in. There is almost no audible interference from outside of that room. In this way, the wider context of his room is erased from the work and the work itself can be read as an acoustic study of interior physical and material space alone. However, in a room such as mine in Indiranagar, exterior actors cannot be kept from interfering with the process. The influx of exterior sounds straining against the threshold of interior space become audible and even emphasised through the act of re-recording. These external actors interfere with the otherwise totalising effect of the room on my voice, leading to a different result from that of Lucier’s. I understand this as the insistence of my context towards being heard (or acknowledged, rather). Through this process, the permeability of urban domestic spaces in Bangalore can be highlighted through listening.
